Context

Google introduces
Core Web Vitals

Google announced Core Web Vitals, a standardised set of metrics for measuring real-world user experience: Largest Contentful Paint, First Input Delay, Cumulative Layout Shift.


For Raygun, an error and performance monitoring platform built for engineering teams, CWV was a natural fit for our Real User Monitoring product. The question wasn't whether to build it, it was how to position it.

02 / 13
The initial hypothesis

The borrowed
frame

Google's announcement framed CWV primarily as a search ranking signal, which makes sense given their product. That narrative spread fast across the industry, from direct competitors, developer publications, and more.

"We highly recommend site owners achieve good Core Web Vitals for success with Search... This, along with other page experience aspects, aligns with what our core ranking systems seek to reward."
Google Search Central

When the company that invented the metrics tells you they affect search rankings, it's easy to lead with that. And we did, along with everyone else.

Our initial messaging
"Improve your Core Web Vitals to protect your search rankings"

Accurate. But we hadn't asked whether it was right for our audience.

03 / 13
Defining the ICP

Who actually
buys Raygun?

Raygun is a multi-product platform giving engineering teams visibility into the health of their software, with three products: crash reporting, APM, and Real User Monitoring. CWV was a natural fit specifically for RUM, which focused on frontend performance. But the buying dynamic was more complex than a single persona.

User
Frontend developer
Uses Raygun RUM day-to-day. Drowning in metrics — CWV gave them a clear north star for what to fix and why. And beyond prioritisation, developers take pride in their craft. A fast, seamless experience matters to them personally.
Buyer
CTO / TDM
Signs off on the tool. Cares about engineering ROI, dev team efficiency, and metrics they report upward: bounce rate, conversion rate, session length. CWV also gives them a shared language across engineering, product and marketing.
Stakeholder
Marketing / website owner
Often owns the website and cares about SEO. This is why the SEO framing was initially tempting, but they don't buy the tool or use it. Optimising for their frame was the mistake.

The SEO angle wasn't wrong, it was just aimed at the wrong person in the room.

04 / 13
Insights to positioning

Reading the
conversation

Because CWV came from Google, there was a rich body of public conversation about these metrics. Rather than starting from scratch, we mapped what was being said, and who was saying it.

Developers
Focused on implementation, debugging tools, real-user data vs synthetic. Wanted to know what to fix and how. Almost no mention of SEO.
Business stakeholders
Cared about bounce rate, conversion rate, revenue. SEO stayed in the conversation as a supporting benefit, but leading with it was the wrong move. Business impact was what actually moved them.
53%
of mobile users abandon a page that takes longer than 3 seconds to load
Google / SOASTA research
"If you want to argue for Core Web Vitals improvements, that's hard to do for SEO. However, you can make a case for it for user experience."
Patrick Stox, Ahrefs - Technical SEO lead
05 / 13
The pivot

Same metrics.
Different frame.

Internal conversations and early adopter feedback confirmed the mismatch. Developers didn't mention search rankings once - they talked about user experience. So we reframed. Here's what that looked like in practice.

Developer's guide — UX frame
"Developers are on the front lines. With more metrics available than ever before, it has become increasingly difficult to know where to focus. Google has stepped in to provide an answer."
Prioritisation problem. Google as the north star. No mention of SEO or business outcomes.
CTO's guide — business frame
"The goal isn't to create more development work; it's to motivate everyone to think about performance from the user's point of view."
Speaks directly to dev team efficiency and ROI. Reframes CWV as an alignment tool, not a compliance burden.
"Positioning isn't just about what's true — it's about what's true for the person you're talking to."
06 / 13
Stakeholder alignment

Cross-functional
ownership

Product: co-created throughout
Looped in from the start. Collaborated on a YouTube explainer with a product team member and a developer, giving the content technical credibility with the core audience.
Sales: trained and equipped
Ran an internal training session on CWV. Produced a one-pager and a new proposal deck slide. As Google updated the spec throughout its first year, kept sales looped in on every change - messaging wasn't a one-time deliverable.
CS: ongoing feedback loop
Weekly syncs with the customer success team fed real customer language back into the messaging. What were customers actually saying when they talked about CWV? That informed how we iterated.

07 / 13
Testing & iteration

How we validated
the messaging

Primary test channel
LinkedIn A/B ads
LinkedIn is my go-to for fast messaging validation. Run A/B tests tied to specific personas, get signal quickly without waiting for organic data. We tracked both CTR and conversion to signup, not just clicks.
Slower but richer
Landing page experiments
We also ran A/B tests on landing page messaging. Takes longer to reach significance, but gives you conversion data that's closer to the actual business outcome.
Qualitative signal
Weekly CS syncs
Regular conversations with the customer success team surfaced what real customers were saying. Quant tells you what's happening. Qual tells you why. Both matter.
What I trusted most
Signup conversion rate
CTR is a useful leading indicator but it's not the goal. We always pulled through to signup rate - did the message attract the right people, not just more people.
08 / 13
Distribution

Spreading
the word

With messaging validated and two clear persona frames locked in, this was the moment to scale. We'd earned the right to go loud. The developer guide and CTO guide became the content backbone — everything else drove people to them or extended the story for a specific channel.

Owned audience
Performance Matters, Raygun's developer-focused performance newsletter with ~50k subscribers, was a natural home for the launch. Plus the quarterly product newsletter and dedicated launch emails for both guides.
Paid & distribution
LinkedIn PPC targeting both personas with separate creative. Sponsored post with Chris Coyier on CSS-Tricks, putting the message directly in front of developers where they already were.
Product & in-app
Website and in-app banners drove awareness to existing users. Updated the onboarding flow to showcase CWV — so new signups understood the feature's value from day one.
Events & enablement
A live webinar, repurposed on-demand after launch. Internal sales training, one-pager, and a proposal deck slide so the messaging carried through to every sales conversation.
09 / 13
Results & measurement

How we measured
success

The north star was always RUM signups. We tracked whether users had engaged with any CWV content prior to signing up. Not trying to attribute every piece, but understanding the campaign's role in the broader journey.

~30%
CTR lift on LinkedIn ads after messaging pivot
~50
Signups from CSS-Tricks post alone

I'm always cautious about over-attributing. Campaigns like this should be measured holistically. Trying to pin every signup to a single piece of content is a losing battle, and sends you down the wrong rabbit holes.

What didn't work
Repurposed webinar clips as LinkedIn ads
We tried clipping the webinar for paid social. Performance was poor. The format didn't translate - webinar content is built for an engaged audience, not a cold scroll. In hindsight, video ads need to be made bespoke for the channel from the start.

11 / 13
How my approach has evolved

What I'd do
differently now

Earlier validation
Talk to customers first
Run customer conversations before committing to a messaging direction. The UX vs SEO insight was available — we just didn't seek it out early enough.
Faster signal
Test on LinkedIn from day one
I'd treat paid channels as a research tool from the start, not a post-launch check. Fast, persona-targeted, and directly tied to signup conversion.
Today's toolkit
Interactive demo
I'd now build a live demo app alongside the content — something developers can interact with, not just read about. Immediate proof of value for a technical audience.
The core lesson
Don't borrow someone else's frame
Accurate isn't the same as relevant. Always ask: is this framing right for the person we're talking to, or just the person who invented the thing?
